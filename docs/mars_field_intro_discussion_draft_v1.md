# MARS-FIELD Introduction and Discussion Draft v1

## Introduction

Protein engineering increasingly relies on computational design pipelines that combine structural analysis, sequence-derived priors, and machine-learning proposal engines. In practice, however, these components are often assembled as loosely connected modules. Structure-aware generators, profile priors, ancestral sequence reconstructions, retrieval-based templates, and engineering heuristics are frequently used in parallel or in succession, with the final decision made by a hand-tuned ranking function. This modularity has been useful for rapid iteration, but it has also created a conceptual gap: many pipelines behave as ensembles of partially redundant signals rather than as a unified learning system.

This gap is particularly limiting in realistic engineering settings, where the design objective is not merely sequence plausibility. Engineering-relevant design requires balancing backbone compatibility, evolutionary tolerability, ancestral recovery, retrieval-supported local motifs, and context-specific constraints such as oxidation risk, exposed flexibility, or environment-conditioned stress goals. These signals are heterogeneous in scale, reliability, and semantics. As a result, naïve combinations often collapse into either heuristic score stacking or generator voting, neither of which provides a satisfying algorithmic formulation.

We developed `MARS-FIELD` to address this problem. The central idea is to treat protein engineering not as a competition among candidate generators, but as an evidence-to-sequence control problem. In `MARS-FIELD`, geometric, phylogenetic, ancestral, retrieval-based, and environment-conditioned signals are projected into a shared residue field over the design positions. This field is the native decision object of the method: it defines residue-wise preferences and pairwise couplings, which are then used both to evaluate incumbent candidates and to decode new ones under engineering constraints. In this view, external generators and priors are not independent voters. They are evidence streams that shape a common residue-level decision space.

The present implementation takes a step beyond our earlier engineering approximations in two ways. First, ancestry and retrieval are no longer treated as isolated recommendation tables, but are represented as learned branches with memory structure inside the neural controller. Second, the neural branch is no longer restricted to reranking. The main benchmark path now includes a neural field decoder that generates candidates from the shared residue field itself. This makes the system meaningfully closer to an end-to-end field-controller architecture, even though full joint optimization of proposal generation, field construction, and decoding remains a future step.

Our goal in this work is therefore not to claim a perfectly end-to-end protein design foundation model. Rather, it is to demonstrate that a unified evidence-to-sequence controller can be built in a way that is mechanistically interpretable, benchmark-stable, and capable of decode-time neural proposal generation. We evaluate `MARS-FIELD` on a twelve-target benchmark spanning ten protein families and show that the final controller remains globally stable after neural decoder integration, improves paired policy score on most targets, and introduces retained novel neural-decoder candidates on a meaningful subset of the panel.

## Discussion

The main conclusion of this study is that a unified residue-field controller provides a more coherent algorithmic basis for protein engineering than an ensemble of disconnected proposal branches. In the current implementation, `MARS-FIELD` integrates heterogeneous evidence streams into a shared residue field, uses a calibrated neural controller to score candidates, and activates a decode-time neural branch within the main benchmark path. This makes the system substantively different from earlier stack-and-rerank pipelines: the neural branch is now involved not only in candidate scoring, but also in proposal generation.

An important aspect of the current results is stability. Introducing a more learned controller and a decode-time neural branch could easily have destabilized the benchmark. Instead, the final controller improved paired policy score on 9 of 12 targets, decreased it on 3 targets, and produced an approximately neutral mean paired delta. We interpret this as a strong engineering result. The near-neutral mean should not be read as lack of effect; it should be read as evidence that the controller can absorb a more ambitious neural architecture without broad failure. At the same time, the fact that gains outnumber losses indicates that the new architecture is not merely complexity for its own sake.

The neural decoder statistics further support this interpretation. Neural decoding was active on all twelve targets, yet retained novel candidates appeared on only five. We view this selectivity as a virtue. A robust engineering controller should not inject novelty uniformly. Instead, it should selectively exploit neural exploration where the field is well supported and filter it out where it is not. In this sense, `1LBT` is as important as the more visibly successful targets: it shows that the neural field can be active without forcing adoption of unsafe or weak decoded alternatives.

The component ablations also reveal that the current method remains strongly grounded in engineering-relevant constraints. Oxidation-aware evidence and evolutionary context remain among the most influential components of the system, and removing them causes widespread shifts in the top candidate. This observation is important because it shows that the neural branch is not simply overwhelming the rest of the pipeline. Instead, the current controller is best understood as a neuralized integration layer sitting on top of still-essential chemical and evolutionary constraints.

Our results also clarify where the method remains limited. First, the neural field decoder is active and productive, but its retained contributions remain target-dependent. Some targets clearly benefit from decoder-derived alternatives, whereas others remain dominated by incumbent or local candidates. Second, the remaining regressions are concentrated but real. The CLD targets and subtilisin reveal that calibration between high-engineering local optima and stable final policy remains imperfect. Third, the present implementation is still not fully jointly trained across proposal generation, field construction, and decoding. The system is now close enough to call an end-to-end field-controller architecture in an engineering-honest sense, but the final research endpoint would require joint optimization rather than the current staged supervision.

These limitations point directly to the next research steps. The most obvious next direction is to replace proposal-conditioned supervision with tighter joint optimization between the field and the decoder. A second direction is to improve how the controller trades off between high local engineering signal and incumbent stability on stress-test families such as CLD. A third is to make retrieval memory and ancestry branches more directly differentiable with respect to upstream evidence construction, reducing reliance on externally prepared evidence tensors.

Even with these limitations, we believe the present implementation is already useful as both a method paper and an engineering system. Methodologically, it provides a more coherent framing of protein engineering as evidence-conditioned field control. Practically, it offers a stable controller that can absorb neural decode-time generation without losing panel-level benchmark reliability. For these reasons, `MARS-FIELD` should be viewed as a credible bridge between traditional engineering pipelines and future fully joint protein design controllers.
